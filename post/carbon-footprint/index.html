Calculating my personal deep learning carbon footprint

How many pounds of CO2 are my models emitting?

Joseph D. Janizek
Nov 13, 2019 2 min read

Yesterday I saw this MIT Technology Review article article shared on Twitter. The article, which summarizes this recent work on energy consumption in Deep Learning in NLP, points out that training a handful of recent state-of-the-art models in Natural Language Processing can be incredibly energy-intensive. Training a Transformer with neural architecture search, for example, emits 626,000 pounds of carbon dioxide – “nearly five times the lifetime emissions of the average American car.”

As someone who trains quite a few deep learning models for my research, I was obviously curious to see how the energy consumption of my models stacked up. As the lead author of the paper points out, “training a single model is the minimum amount of work you can do.” Most deep learning research involves training, tuning, and re-training models repeatedly while prototyping and searching for optimal model settings. Therefore, instead of trying to estimate how much energy is consumed during the training of a single one of my models, I instead wanted to estimate my energy consumption during an average week of research. In an average week I’d guess that I probably have models training on 1-2 Nvidia GeForce RTX 2080 GPUs about 50% of the time. A quick check of nvidia-smi shows that each GPU pulls around 200W during training.

2 GPUs, each running half of the time, leads to around 168 hours of GPU usage a week. That puts my weekly energy consumption for deep learning at 33.6 kWh.
